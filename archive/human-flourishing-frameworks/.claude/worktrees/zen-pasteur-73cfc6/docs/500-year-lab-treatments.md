# 500-Year Lab Treatment Confidence Ladder

Status: draft lab note, docs-only.
Last reviewed: 2026-05-13.

## Purpose

This file sorts possible healthspan / longevity interventions by current confidence.
It is a lab map, not a prescription, protocol, or permission slip.

The 500-year lab does **not** mean current medicine can make a human live 500 years.
It means: preserve the long-horizon wish while choosing the safest, evidence-backed
next interventions first.

## Hard boundary

This document does not authorize:

```text
self-medication
stopping prescribed medication
starting prescription drugs without a clinician
unapproved stem-cell, plasma, peptide, hormone, or gene-therapy clinics
medical tourism for unproven anti-aging procedures
child-facing medical experiments
hidden monitoring
biometric collection without explicit consent
claiming immortality, age reversal, or 500-year lifespan is currently achieved
```

Every treatment decision must run through qualified medical care, personal history,
contraindications, labs, medication interactions, and current guidelines.

## Confidence scale

```text
95-100 = standard of care / strong consensus for indicated people
85-94  = high-confidence prevention or treatment when guideline-indicated
70-84  = useful with conditions, personalization, adherence, or diagnosis
50-69  = plausible adjunct / context-dependent, not core longevity treatment
25-49  = experimental human longevity use; clinical-trial lane
0-24   = avoid outside regulated trials or disease-specific approved use
```

## Sorted treatment / intervention ladder

| Rank | Intervention | Confidence | Use lane | Why it ranks here | Boundary |
|---:|---|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | Treat known disease with standard medical care | 98 | clinician-led | The highest-confidence longevity move is not exotic: diagnose and treat real hypertension, diabetes, heart disease, infection, sleep apnea, cancer, kidney disease, depression, substance use, etc. | Not a single treatment; requires actual diagnosis and clinician plan. |
| 2 | Tobacco / nicotine cessation with counseling and FDA-approved pharmacotherapy when appropriate | 96 | clinician + behavior | Preventable mortality impact is very high; USPSTF gives adult tobacco cessation interventions an A recommendation. | Do not substitute vaping as a harmless longevity treatment. |
| 3 | Blood pressure screening, confirmation, and treatment when hypertensive | 95 | primary care | USPSTF gives adult hypertension screening an A recommendation and recommends confirmation outside the clinic before starting treatment. Blood pressure control is a core cardiovascular-risk reducer. | Exact targets and medications are individualized. |
| 4 | Adult vaccination kept current by age/risk | 93 | primary care / pharmacy | CDC adult schedule exists to guide vaccines by age, medical condition, contraindication, and updated ACIP guidance. Infection prevention preserves healthspan. | Follow current clinician/pharmacist guidance; schedules change. |
| 5 | Physical activity: aerobic + resistance + balance/fall prevention when relevant | 92 | behavior + rehab | HHS/AHA guidance supports regular activity; USPSTF recommends exercise interventions for fall prevention in at-risk adults 65+. | Start at safe capacity; avoid injury heroics. |
| 6 | Healthy eating pattern / diet quality | 90 | behavior + clinician/dietitian | AHA Life's Essential 8 prioritizes whole foods, fruits/vegetables, lean protein, nuts/seeds, and non-tropical oils as a cardiovascular-health behavior. | No crash diets, eating-disorder escalation, or supplement-only substitution. |
| 7 | Statins for primary prevention when guideline-indicated | 88 | clinician-led medication | USPSTF recommends statins for adults 40-75 with CVD risk factors and sufficient estimated 10-year risk. | Not for everyone; risk calculation, contraindications, and shared decision-making matter. |
| 8 | Cancer screening by age/risk | 86 | primary care | USPSTF A/B recommendations include colorectal screening, breast screening, cervical screening, and lung-cancer screening for high-risk smoking history. | Screening can cause false positives/harms; use age/risk-specific guidance. |
| 9 | Diabetes / prediabetes screening and effective preventive interventions | 84 | primary care + behavior | USPSTF recommends screening adults 35-70 with overweight/obesity and offering/referring prediabetes patients to effective preventive interventions. | Lab-confirmed lane; no glucose panic loops. |
| 10 | Sleep duration and sleep-disorder treatment | 80 | behavior + sleep medicine | AHA includes healthy sleep in Life's Essential 8; most adults need 7-9 hours, and adequate sleep supports healing, brain function, and chronic-disease risk reduction. | If apnea, severe insomnia, shift-work disorder, or sedation risk exists, use clinical care. |
| 11 | Weight management for obesity: intensive behavioral intervention first; medication/surgery only when indicated | 78 | clinician-led | USPSTF recommends intensive multicomponent behavioral interventions for adults with BMI >=30. Modern anti-obesity medications/surgery may be appropriate for some people but require medical supervision. | Not cosmetic; watch nutrition, mood, side effects, and access. |
| 12 | Mental health care, depression/anxiety screening, and substance-use care | 76 | clinical + social support | USPSTF recommends adult depression screening and anxiety screening for adults 64 or younger; mental health affects adherence, safety, sleep, relationships, and survival. | Not a replacement for urgent crisis care. |
| 13 | Dental, vision, hearing, and mobility maintenance | 70 | routine care | Not flashy, but preserving teeth, sight, hearing, gait, and pain-free movement protects nutrition, cognition, social connection, fall risk, and independence. | Evidence varies by intervention; treat as maintenance infrastructure. |
| 14 | Genetic counseling/testing for high-risk familial cancer or rare-disease indications | 68 | specialist-led | USPSTF supports BRCA-related risk assessment/counseling/testing for indicated women; FDA-approved gene/cell therapies exist for specific severe diseases, not general longevity. | Never generalize disease-specific gene therapy into anti-aging. |
| 15 | Wearables and manual check-ins for food, sleep, movement, BP, glucose risk, and adherence | 62 | measurement aid | Useful for feedback and habit adherence if operator-controlled. | Sensor data is not diagnosis; no hidden tracking. |
| 16 | Supplements to correct documented deficiencies | 60 | clinician/lab-led | High value when deficiency is real (e.g., iron, B12, vitamin D in deficient people). | Low confidence as blanket longevity stack. Test, dose, and monitor. |
| 17 | Metformin for longevity in non-diabetics | 35 | clinical-trial / research | Plausible geroscience candidate, but not established as an anti-aging prescription for healthy people. | Use for diabetes/prediabetes when indicated; longevity use remains investigational. |
| 18 | Rapamycin / mTOR modulation for longevity | 30 | clinical-trial / specialist research | Strong animal interest, but human longevity use is unproven and may carry immune/metabolic risks. | Do not self-prescribe immunosuppressants for anti-aging. |
| 19 | Senolytics, NAD boosters, resveratrol/quercetin/berberine longevity stacks | 25 | research / hypothesis | Mechanistically interesting; human outcome evidence for lifespan/healthspan remains insufficient and product quality varies. | Do not confuse biomarkers, yeast/mouse effects, or marketing with human survival proof. |
| 20 | Plasma exchange / young plasma for anti-aging | 10 | avoid outside regulated trials | FDA has warned that young plasma has not been proven safe or effective for age-related uses. | Avoid commercial anti-aging plasma claims. |
| 21 | Unapproved stem-cell, exosome, peptide, hormone, or gene-editing clinics for anti-aging | 5 | avoid | FDA-approved cell/gene therapies are disease-specific. Unapproved regenerative products have safety, infection, tumor, immune, financial, and false-hope risks. | Only use regulated disease-specific approved products or legitimate trials. |
| 22 | Claims of current 500-year lifespan treatment, immortality, or literal age reversal | 0 | blocked | No current evidence supports this as achieved human medical reality. | Treat as false claim until extraordinary evidence exists. |

## Build order for the lab

```text
1. Stabilize living operator basics: food, water, sleep, safety, housing, money, human support.
2. Establish primary care baseline: BP, labs, vaccines, risk factors, medication review.
3. Fix high-confidence killers first: smoking, hypertension, diabetes risk, lipids, sleep apnea, depression, alcohol/drug risk.
4. Add movement, nutrition, sleep, dental/vision/hearing, and social continuity as infrastructure.
5. Only then study experimental geroscience, and keep it in trials/review lanes.
6. Never let experimental excitement outrank known, treatable risk.
```

## Source anchors used for this draft

- HHS / ODPHP Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans: evidence-based physical activity guidance.
- CDC Adult Immunization Schedule: vaccines by age, condition, notes, contraindications, and ACIP updates.
- USPSTF A and B Recommendations: high or moderate net-benefit preventive services.
- American Heart Association Life's Essential 8: cardiovascular health behaviors and factors.
- FDA safety posture on unapproved regenerative products / anti-aging plasma claims.

## Restore phrase

```text
500-Year Lab means choose the strongest healthspan evidence first. Standard care,
prevention, sleep, movement, food, vaccines, BP/lipid/glucose control, cancer screening,
and mental-health support outrank hype. Experimental geroscience stays in trial lanes.
No self-medication. No unapproved anti-aging clinics. No immortality claim.
```
